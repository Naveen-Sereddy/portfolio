Problem

The store relied on word-of-mouth, but first-timers bounced before they understood what they were walking into.

Customers didn't understand the liquidation pricing model, the product categories, or how the store operated. The business had no centralized digital presence — just social media posts and a physical location. Most design decisions had to answer one question: what does a first-time customer need to know before driving to the store?

The confusion wasn't about the store itself. It was about explaining an unfamiliar business model — liquidation pricing — to people who had never experienced it.

Role + Scope

Full ownership: UX, UI, front-end, SEO, and deployment.

Stakeholder

Worked directly with the owner to extract operational rules, common customer questions, and business goals.

UX + Content

Customer question analysis, content architecture, information hierarchy for first-time visitors.

UI + Build

Responsive design, React + Tailwind CSS implementation, SEO metadata, Vercel deployment.

Acquisition Funnels

Facebook and Marketplace integration — the primary referral channels for new store visitors.

Constraints: Live client with real operational requirements. Content had to be accurate to actual store operations — pricing tiers, hours, categories — not hypothetical. SEO needed to capture local search intent for a physical location.

Process

Less confusion first. Features second.

1

Stakeholder Discussions

Interviewed the owner to surface the most common customer questions: "How does the pricing work?", "What kind of stuff do you carry?", "When are you open?" These became the site's content backbone.

2

Content Architecture

Structured the site around first-timer needs: pricing model first, then inventory types, then hours and location, then social proof. Promotional features (claw machine, weekly deals) added as secondary hooks.

3

Responsive Design

Built mobile-first — most visitors arrive from Facebook or Marketplace on a phone. Color-tag pricing system designed as a scannable visual reference, not a text table.

4

Build + Deployment

Implemented with React and Tailwind CSS. Configured SEO metadata, Open Graph tags for social sharing, and structured data for local search. Deployed to Vercel with a custom domain.

Key Decisions

How I resolved the main design tensions.

Pricing system before product catalog.

Customer confusion about liquidation pricing was the leading reason people avoided the store. I led with the color-tag pricing explainer instead of a typical product grid, because understanding the model is a prerequisite for wanting to shop.

React + Tailwind over a CMS platform.

The owner needed direct control over promotional content and SEO without CMS overhead or recurring subscription costs. A static React build gave full control, fast load times, and no platform lock-in.

Facebook and Marketplace funnels on the homepage.

Stakeholder discussions confirmed social media drove the majority of first-time visits. I integrated those referral paths directly into the site's primary section rather than burying them in a contact page.

Outcome

Live, shipped, and in front of customers.

The site launched at binsanddeals.com and serves as the primary digital presence for the store. Customers arriving from Facebook or Marketplace can now understand the pricing model, check hours, and locate the store before visiting. The color-tag pricing system reduced the most common pre-visit confusion.

No analytics budget for a single-location store, so I tracked what the owner and staff could observe directly: repeat questions at the counter and phone, and self-reported referral source from new customers in the first month.

-78%Pre-visit confusion calls (14–16/wk → 2–4/wk)
+22%New-customer foot traffic (~190/mo → ~230/mo)

Reflection

What I'd do differently.

1

I'd wire up basic analytics before launch, not after. Call counts and staff-reported foot traffic are honest numbers, but they're not provable trend lines — a lightweight pageview and referral setup from day one would let me show the trend, not just describe it.

2

I'd test the pricing explainer with a few actual first-time customers before shipping, not just the owner and staff. Internal validation caught the obvious gaps, but the person the whole site is designed for (someone who's never set foot in a liquidation store) never saw it before launch.
