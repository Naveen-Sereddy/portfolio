The Problem

Patients juggled four disconnected portals and still couldn't find basic information.

The existing patient portal scattered appointments, prescriptions, medical records, and billing across separate sections with no shared navigation. Patients called the support line for simple tasks. The mental model didn't match real-world healthcare needs.

This wasn't a visual problem. It was a structural one. The portal was built as four separate tools — and patients experienced it exactly that way.

Research

Three patterns that shaped every decision that followed.

User interviews, support-call analysis, and competitive review across five existing healthcare portals — anchored on two archetypes at opposite ends of tech literacy: Sarah, a mobile-first patient, and Robert, an older caregiver.

01

Patients treat the portal as one product

Every interview participant described "the portal" as a single thing — but experienced it as four disconnected tools. Navigation drop-off was highest when users tried to switch between service areas.

02

Appointments are the front door

Scheduling and managing visits was the most frequent entry point. Yet the appointment flow required the most clicks and had the most dead-end navigation paths of any section in the portal.

03

Stress amplifies every usability flaw

Patients access health portals during anxious moments — before a procedure, after a diagnosis, when a prescription runs out. Cognitive load was already high before any interaction began. Clarity wasn't a nice-to-have.

Design Decisions

Six decisions the journey map forced.

Mapping Sarah's path from "I should see my cardiologist" to a confirmed visit surfaced two specific friction points. Every decision below traces back to a moment where the old portal made an anxious task harder than it needed to be.

01

In-network by default, sorted by fit

Sarah's real friction: too many results, in-network status unclear, choice paralysis. The redesign defaults every search to in-network and sorts by soonest availability plus best match — not an alphabetical wall of providers.

02

Real availability, not a guessing game

Her second friction: few slots fit a 9-to-5 schedule, and availability was hard to parse. Evening and telehealth slots are highlighted up front, with real open times shown before she commits to a provider.

03

Plain language, not clinical jargon

"Understand lab results in plain language, not codes" was Sarah's stated goal. Test results carry a plain-language "Reviewed" status instead of raw lab codes — the same information, without requiring a medical degree to parse it.

04

One accent color, spent only on action

Blue appears exactly where a decision is live — a primary button, an active nav item, a selected date. Everything else stays neutral, so the one thing that needs attention doesn't have to compete with brand color for it.

05

44px targets, built for shaky hands

Robert's pain point: afraid of clicking the wrong button and breaking something. Every interactive control spans a minimum 44×44px hit area with 8px of clear space around it — comfortable for older caregivers and anyone with reduced motor precision.

06

A keyboard path, not a visual afterthought

Tab order follows the visual reading order exactly, a skip-link sits first in the DOM, and every icon-only control carries a descriptive aria-label. Verified with an actual Tab-and-VoiceOver pass, not just a color-contrast checker.

Scoped Out, On Purpose

Robert needed two accounts. He got one.

Not everything in the research made it into this version — and that's a decision worth being honest about.

The caregiver dual-account gap

Robert's persona goal was explicit: "keep his and Elena's appointments in one organized place." His real pain point was just as direct — switching between his own account and hers was confusing, on top of an interface he already found dense and hard to read.

Designing real caregiver access — a second identity with permissioned visibility into a spouse's records, not just a shared login — would have changed several information-architecture decisions documented here: how the dashboard greets the user, how appointments get attributed, how billing splits across two people. That's a redesign of the account model, not a feature added on top of one.

I scoped this version around a single-patient account and documented the gap rather than bolt on a shortcut that would have undermined the "one calm, organized place" goal for every other patient. It's the first thing in the reflection below, and it's the most consequential thing I'd build next.

Design Evolution

From lo-fi to final — the dashboard.

The dashboard shows the clearest progression. KPI cards above the fold, two content blocks below — the layout decision was made at wireframe stage and never changed.

Lo-Fi

Two-column structure below the KPI row — Upcoming Appointments left, Health Summary right. Confirmed at wireframe stage that patients needed both without choosing one over the other.

Final UI

Visual system applied — stat cards, accent-led appointment rows, a vitals grid. The structure from the wireframe is unchanged, because the hierarchy was already solved.

Research, On the Screens

Every numbered dot traces back to a friction point.

The dashboard and provider search carry the most research weight. Annotated below with the specific finding behind each element.

1

Pending balance sits beside unread messages, not buried in Billing. Insight 02 — appointments and money are both front-door tasks. Sarah shouldn't need to leave the dashboard to know she owes $248.

2

Confirmed, Telehealth, and Pending are visible without opening a row. Insight 03 — stress amplifies friction. A patient scanning for "what do I need to do next" gets the answer in the list, not behind a click.

3

Vitals carry a "Confirmed" status, the same pattern as appointments. One visual language for "this is settled" reduces the number of things a stressed patient has to learn to read this screen.

4 5 6
4

Insurance filter defaults to a live decision, not a dead-end dropdown. Decision 01 — the journey map's biggest friction was in-network status being unclear. Filtering by insurance is a first-class control, not buried in Advanced Search.

5

Rating and years of experience sit directly under the name. Choice paralysis came from too many undifferentiated results — a trust signal at a glance narrows the decision without opening every profile.

6

Book is one tap from the search results, not three screens deep. Decision 02 — real availability shown up front means Sarah commits to a slot without a separate "check availability" round trip.

Final UI

One system. Five unified flows.

Appointments, records, prescriptions, billing, and profile all share one navigation model and one component language. Consistency reduces cognitive load when patients are already under stress.

Reflection

What I'd do differently.

1

I'd include caregiver flows from the start. Research mentioned older patients often had a family member navigating on their behalf — but I designed entirely for the patient as primary user. A caregiver access model would meaningfully change several IA decisions.

2

The mobile experience was designed after desktop. Given that appointment check-ins and prescription refills happen most often on phones, I'd reverse that order — mobile-first wireframing, then scale up to desktop.

3

Accessibility was built into components, but I didn't test with screen reader users during wireframing. I'd add at least one assistive-technology session early in the lo-fi stage, not only as a final audit before handoff.

Early Sketch Notes

Paper first. Before committing to anything.

Hand-drawn sketches let me stress-test layout decisions without getting attached to pixels. Each sketch answered one structural question before moving to digital.

Split-screen sign-in

Brand copy ("Your health, simplified.") and trust signals — HIPAA Compliant, 4.9 Rating, 200K+ Patients — on the left means new patients understand what they're signing into before entering credentials. For a health portal, trust has to arrive before the password field.

Dashboard with KPI cards above the fold

Upcoming appointments, active prescriptions, pending balance, and unread messages surface as cards before any scroll — the four things patients check first. Patients under stress shouldn't need to hunt.

Appointments list with inline actions

Status tabs (All / Upcoming / Past / Cancelled) at the top — not in a dropdown. Telehealth appointments show a "Join" button directly in the list row, so starting a virtual visit never requires a detail screen first.

Calendar with persistent day-detail panel

Selecting a date reveals the appointment card and a "Book This Day" button in a panel on the right — without navigating away from the calendar. Checking and booking on the same day stays in one context.
