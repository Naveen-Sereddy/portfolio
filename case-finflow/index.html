The Problem

Three roles. One broken tool. Nobody could finish their task.

Employees got lost in category dropdowns and abandoned mid-submission. Managers couldn't see team context before approving — so approvals stalled. Admins had no consolidated view of spend or compliance status, making month-end reconciliation a manual scramble.

Usability testing confirmed what stakeholder interviews suggested: the tool was technically functional but practically unusable under real work conditions. The problem wasn't features — it was task structure.

Research

What users told us before a single screen was drawn.

Stakeholder interviews and user sessions across all three roles surfaced three patterns that shaped every design decision that followed.

01

Submission happens at the moment of purchase

Employees tried to submit expenses immediately after spending — not days later at a desk. The tool wasn't built for mobile capture, so receipts went missing and submissions piled up.

02

Managers approve blindly

Approvals arrived with no spend context — no team totals, no budget headroom, no policy flags. Managers either rubber-stamped everything or created bottlenecks requesting more information.

03

Admins do month-end manually

Finance admins exported raw data to spreadsheets to understand total spend, policy exceptions, and reimbursement status. The tool had no reconciliation layer — just a transaction list.

Early Sketches

Paper first. Before committing to anything.

Rough sketches let me test structure without pixel polish getting in the way. Each one answered a specific question about task flow before moving to digital.

Mobile-first home screen Surfaced "Snap receipt" as the primary action — because research showed most employee submissions happen at the point of purchase, not at a desk. The home screen needed to serve that moment.
Split-screen sign-in Brand messaging ("The whole company's spend, in flow.") on the left means SSO authentication doesn't feel cold or purely transactional. New users absorb the product value proposition before they even log in.
Two-column expense form Form fields on the left, receipt drop zone on the right — so users can fill metadata and upload evidence in parallel. The old tool forced sequential steps, which caused drop-off halfway through.
Inline approvals queue Row-based list with Approve/Reject per row — managers can process a full queue without opening individual submissions. Designed for the manager's actual task: moving fast through a backlog, not reviewing one expense at a time.
Lo-Fi Wireframes

Digital wireframes. Decisions locked in.

Moving from sketch to lo-fi is where task structure gets tested. Each wireframe answered whether the layout actually let users complete their primary task without confusion.

Approval preview before submitting Added approval routing preview in the right panel — employees see exactly who will review the expense before hitting submit. Removes the anxiety of "where does this go?" that caused hesitation and abandoned forms.
KPIs above the fold, queue one scroll away Four KPI cards (team spend, pending approvals, budget status, flagged items) appear before any interaction. Managers see the number they need in under two seconds — then scroll to act on the queue below.
Status as the primary sort dimension Tabs (All / Pending / Approved / Flagged / Rejected) replace dropdown filtering — because status is the first thing every role filters by. Surface-level navigation beats a buried filter panel for the most common action.
Batch payout dispatch in one action Finance admins can select multiple reimbursements and schedule them in one slide-over panel — instead of approving one at a time. Designed for the admin's biggest pain: month-end takes hours, not minutes.
Design Evolution

From sketch to final — one key flow.

The new expense submission flow shows the clearest progression. Each stage made the task structure more precise without changing the core intent established in the first sketch.

Sketch

Two-column layout, receipt zone right, no sequential steps. Rough but structurally sound.

Lo-Fi

Sidebar navigation added. Approval preview panel appears — the insight from user interviews that solved mid-form abandonment.

Final UI

Visual system applied. Structure unchanged from lo-fi — because the layout decision was already right.

Final UI

One design system. Three role views.

Shared component primitives — tables, filters, status chips, approval actions — adapted to each role's primary task. Consistency across roles reduced the learning curve when users switched contexts.

"We didn't just polish screens. We changed the task structure so users could finish faster, with fewer errors, and less cognitive fatigue — and measured it."

Design outcome · Maze usability testing

Outcome

One system, three roles, measurable improvement.

The final design unified admin, manager, and employee into one consistent platform with role-specific dashboards and shared component primitives. Maze usability testing measured task completion lifting from 73% to 89% across the core expense workflows. Error rate and time-to-complete dropped in every tested flow.

Reflection

What I'd do differently.

1

I'd test the mobile snap-receipt flow earlier. It was sketched in the first round but deprioritized during wireframing — the most common real-world submission scenario got the least design attention.

2

The three role dashboards share components but were designed in sequence rather than in parallel. Designing them side-by-side from the start would have surfaced shared layout decisions earlier and reduced rework.

3

Task completion rate was the right metric — but I'd add time-on-task as a secondary measure from the first usability round, not just the final one. Speed matters as much as completion for a daily-use tool.
